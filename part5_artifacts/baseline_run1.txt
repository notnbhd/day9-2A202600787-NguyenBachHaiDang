When a company breaches a contract and engages in tax evasion, it faces a range of legal and regulatory consequences. Here’s a comprehensive overview of the implications under contract law, tax law, and regulatory compliance:

### 1. Contract Law Consequences

**A. Breach of Contract**
- A breach occurs when one party fails to fulfill its obligations under the contract without a lawful excuse. Breaches can be classified as:
  - **Material Breach**: Significant enough to allow the non-breaching party to terminate the contract and seek damages.
  - **Minor Breach**: Less significant, allowing for damages but not termination.

**B. Remedies for Breach**
- The non-breaching party may pursue several remedies:
  - **Damages**: 
    - **Expectation Damages**: Compensate for what the non-breaching party expected to gain from the contract.
    - **Consequential Damages**: Cover losses that were foreseeable at the time of the contract.
    - **Liquidated Damages**: Pre-agreed amounts specified in the contract.
  - **Specific Performance**: A court order requiring the breaching party to fulfill their contractual obligations.
  - **Rescission**: Nullifying the contract entirely.

### 2. Tax Law Consequences

**A. Tax Evasion**
- Tax evasion is the illegal act of not paying taxes owed, often through misrepresentation. It is a criminal offense under the Internal Revenue Code (IRC).

**B. Consequences of Tax Evasion**
- **Civil Penalties**: The IRS can impose significant fines, including:
  - **Failure-to-File Penalty**: Generally 5% of unpaid taxes per month, up to 25%.
  - **Failure-to-Pay Penalty**: Typically 0.5% of unpaid taxes per month.
- **Criminal Penalties**: Can lead to felony charges, with penalties including:
  - **Fines**: Up to $250,000 for individuals and $500,000 for corporations.
  - **Imprisonment**: Up to 5 years in prison.

**C. Regulatory Scrutiny**
- Tax evasion may lead to audits and investigations by the IRS or state tax authorities, increasing penalties and damaging the company's reputation.

### 3. Liability Exposure

**A. Joint Liability**
- Company officers and directors may face personal liability if they were complicit in the breach or tax evasion.

**B. Shareholder Actions**
- Shareholders may initiate derivative actions against management for failing to act in the company’s best interests, especially if these actions lead to financial losses.

### 4. Regulatory Compliance Considerations

**A. Jurisdictional Agencies**
- **Contract Breach**: Typically handled in civil courts, but may attract DOJ attention if fraud is involved.
- **Tax Matters**: Oversight by the IRS and state tax authorities.

**B. Mitigating Factors**
- **Voluntary Disclosure**: Companies may reduce penalties by voluntarily disclosing breaches or tax issues.
- **Cooperation**: Full cooperation with investigations can be a mitigating factor.
- **Remediation**: Corrective actions, such as paying owed taxes or compensating affected parties, can help reduce penalties.

**C. Cross-Border Regulatory Exposure**
- Multinational companies may face scrutiny from foreign regulatory bodies if actions violate local laws, including implications under the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA).

### Conclusion
In summary, a company that breaches a contract while engaging in tax evasion faces significant legal and regulatory repercussions, including potential damages under contract law and severe civil and criminal penalties under tax law.
