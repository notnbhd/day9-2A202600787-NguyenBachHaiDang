#### 1. Breach of Contract

**Legal Consequences:**
- **Damages:** The non-breaching party can seek:
  - **Compensatory Damages:** Covering direct losses from the breach.
  - **Consequential Damages:** For foreseeable losses at the time of the contract.
  - **Liquidated Damages:** Predefined amounts specified in the contract.
- **Court Orders:** Courts may order:
  - **Specific Performance:** Compelling the breaching party to fulfill obligations.
  - **Rescission:** Voiding the contract and restoring parties to their original positions.

**Statutory Framework:** Governed by the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) for sales of goods, which outlines specific remedies.

**Case Law Principles:** Courts emphasize good faith and fair dealing, as seen in landmark cases.

#### 2. Tax Evasion

**Legal Consequences:**
- **Civil Penalties:** The IRS can impose fines for unpaid taxes, with penalties exceeding 20% of the underpaid amount.
- **Criminal Charges:** Tax evasion is a felony, leading to fines up to $250,000 and imprisonment for up to five years.

**Statutory Framework:** Governed by the Internal Revenue Code (IRC), with state laws potentially imposing additional penalties.

**Case Law Principles:** Courts have established that willful blindness to tax obligations can constitute tax evasion.

#### 3. Liability Exposure

**Combined Consequences:**
- Companies may face compounded liabilities from both breaches, attracting legal actions from non-breaching parties and government penalties for tax evasion.

**Reputational Damage:** Both breaches can severely harm a company’s reputation, leading to loss of customers and business partnerships.

**Increased Scrutiny:** Involvement in both breaches may lead to heightened regulatory scrutiny, resulting in audits and investigations.
